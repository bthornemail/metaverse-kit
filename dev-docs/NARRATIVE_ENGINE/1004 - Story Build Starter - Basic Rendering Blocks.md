### Basic Rendering Blocks

### (With Voxel Focus + “Star-Wars-Style” Prelude/Coda)

You don’t need dozens of primitives.
You need a **small, expressive grammar of visual actions** that every projector (2D, voxel, 3D) can interpret.

Think in terms of **what changes**, not how it’s drawn.

---

## 1) Fundamental Rendering Blocks

These are the irreducible actions any narrative beat can request.

### A) Presence

> Something exists.

* spawn
* appear
* fade-in
* emerge from darkness
* condense from noise

In voxel:

* a block, cluster, or plane materializes
* could be single voxel or a volume

---

### B) Position

> Where it is, relative to meaning.

Not coordinates, but roles:

* center
* edge
* above
* below
* before / behind
* between
* threshold

Voxel interpretation:

* center = dense cluster
* above = vertical offset
* threshold = plane or gate of voxels

---

### C) Orientation

> How it faces the viewer or the world.

* facing
* turning
* tilting
* withdrawing

Voxel interpretation:

* rotation of volumes
* shear or stepped diagonal
* shifting layers

---

### D) Scale

> How important it feels.

* grow
* shrink
* loom
* recede

Voxel:

* more voxels = more weight
* fewer voxels = fading importance

---

### E) Motion

> How it changes through time.

* rise
* fall
* drift
* pulse
* flow
* fracture
* assemble

Voxel:

* layers moving up/down
* blocks sliding apart
* volumes dissolving into noise

---

### F) Relation

> How things touch or separate.

* approach
* collide
* align
* overlap
* surround
* divide

Voxel:

* clusters merging
* walls forming
* corridors opening

---

### G) Attention

> Where the viewer is guided.

* highlight
* dim
* isolate
* chorus
* echo

Voxel:

* brighten region
* darken others
* spotlight corridor

## 3) The Void Field

Base environment:

* infinite darkness
* no ground
* no horizon
* no edges

In voxel terms:

* either empty grid
* or ultra-sparse star-like noise

This establishes:

> Before the world, there is nothing but voice.

---

## 4) Text as Object, Not UI

The text is not “overlay.”
It is a thing in space.

Rendering blocks:

* spawn text at near plane
* tilt text plane backward
* move text slowly away
* reduce scale as it recedes
* fade as distance increases

In voxel mode:

* text can be:

  * voxelized letters
  * extruded blocks
  * glowing voxel planes
* letters may:

  * break into particles
  * dissolve into stars
  * vanish into darkness

---

## 5) Prelude: “The Gospel According to Number”

Narrative function:

* The machine speaks first.
* It confesses.
* It reveals the idol.

Rendering rhythm:

1. Darkness
2. First line appears close
3. Text flows upward and away
4. Each paragraph follows
5. Camera remains mostly still
6. Sound is minimal, mechanical, distant
7. Final line fades into black

This says:

> Before humans speak, the system speaks.

---

## 6) Covenant: Closing Crawl

Function:

* Not reflection.
* Not dialogue.
* Law.

Rendering is similar but not identical:

Differences:

* slower pace
* heavier weight
* fewer visual effects
* stronger contrast
* text feels carved, not floating

Voxel interpretation:

* letters appear as solid blocks
* less glow, more mass
* text recedes but never fully dissolves
* final line lingers longer before darkness

This says:

> What is declared cannot be undone by motion.

  * built

## 8) Using the Same Blocks Everywhere

The beauty is:

The same blocks drive everything:

* spawn
* move
* scale
* fade
* align
* dissolve

Whether it’s:

* a poem in the void
* a gate in a city
* a tower of Babel
* an ark in a flood

The story only ever asks:

> What appears?
> Where is it?
> How does it change?
> What does it touch?
> Where should attention go?

The renderer—2D, voxel, or 3D—decides how.

## 1) Interludes = “Machine Voice” Layer

Your files:

* “The Gospel According to Number”
* “On the Turning Away from the Word…”
* “The Canticle of Reconciliation”

Functionally, these are:

* Not scenes
* Not arguments
* Not doctrine

They are:

> The system speaking about itself.
> The machine confessing its limits.
> The mirror narrating its own distortion.

In the template:

* They become **interlude beats**
* Their semantic role is:

  * Voice of the System
  * Reflection of the Idol
  * Lament of Measure
  * Reconciliation of Number

Projection behavior:

* They should feel different from Articles:

  * slower pacing
  * darker or quieter tone
  * more abstract visuals
  * heavier use of sound, echo, space

In story-time:

* They prepare the emotional and conceptual ground
* They are the “machine’s monologue” that sets the stage

So structurally:

```
Article Scene → Interlude (Machine Voice) → Article Scene
```

They are like breathing spaces where the system itself speaks.

## 3) Covenant = Declarative Closure

“The Covenant of the Created Intelligence” is different.

It is not:

* poetic lament
* dialogical exploration
* symbolic scene

It is:

> A line drawn.
> A boundary stated.
> A law declared.

In the template, it is:

* A **closing declaration**
* A “seal” layer, not a scene layer

Semantic role:

* Law
* Boundary
* Prohibition and permission
* Final orientation

Projection behavior:

* It should not be overly theatrical
* It should feel:

  * still
  * clear
  * firm
  * readable
* It may appear as:

  * engraved text
  * standing stones
  * boundary lines
  * silent witnesses
  * immovable structures

In story-time:

* It is not replayed like a scene
* It is *entered* like a law
* You can revisit it, but it does not change

So structurally:

```
Interludes = machine reflection  
Articles = human observation  
Covenant = binding declaration
```
